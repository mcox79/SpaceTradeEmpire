# Epilogue Cards — Design Specification v0

**Status**: CONTENT DRAFT
**Date**: 2026-03-21
**Total**: 10 cards (2 per faction: positive relationship + negative/neutral relationship)
**Format**: Second person, present tense. ~150 words each.

---

## Design Principles

1. **Specific, not generic.** Name places, reference events, describe consequences that could only happen in this game's world.
2. **The faction acts after you leave.** These cards describe what happens to the galaxy after the player's story ends. The player is an observer now.
3. **Positive cards are bittersweet, not triumphant.** Even the best outcomes have costs.
4. **Negative cards are melancholic, not punitive.** The factions are not villains. Their negative outcomes are the natural consequences of being abandoned by someone who could have helped.
5. **Path sensitivity.** Each card nods to the endgame path (Reinforce/Naturalize/Renegotiate) without requiring a specific one.

## Concord

### Card 1: Concord — Positive (Allied Standing)

**"The Last Honest Report"**

The Concord Intelligence Directorate publishes the thread degradation data. All of it. The classified projections, the suppression directives, the internal memos arguing about whether the lie was worth the stability it purchased. They publish it because you showed them what honesty looks like when it is backed by competence instead of fear.

The public reaction is quieter than anyone expected. There are protests, yes. There are resignations on the Council. But the Concord stations keep running, the humanitarian convoys keep flying, and the subsidized Food shipments continue to the Communion waystations that depend on them. The infrastructure holds because the people who built it are still the people who maintain it, and they do not stop doing their jobs because the truth is ugly.

The last report carries a dedication. It reads: "For the pilot who proved that transparency and stability are not opposites." Your name is not attached. Concord does not name its sources. But the Directorate knows. They always did.

### Card 2: Concord — Negative (Hostile/Neutral Standing)

**"The Quiet Collapse"**

Concord does not fall. That would be dramatic, and Concord does not do drama. Instead, the Intelligence Directorate doubles down on suppression. The thread degradation data stays classified. The internal projections get worse. The Council votes to increase patrol budgets by 40% and cut humanitarian convoys by the same amount — security over service, for the first time in Concord's history.

You are not there to see the Food shipments to Waystation Kell slow from weekly to monthly. You are not there when the Stationmaster sends a request for resupply and receives a form letter about "resource reallocation in the current security environment." You are not there when she tells her children that dinner will be smaller tonight, and tomorrow, and for a while.

The lie calcifies. Concord becomes what it always feared becoming: an institution that protects its own survival instead of the people it was built to serve. The thread degradation does not stop. It was never going to stop. But now, no one is looking for alternatives, because looking requires admitting the problem exists, and the Directorate has made admitting the problem a security offense.

The humanitarian convoys were real. That was always the cruelest part.

## Chitin Syndicates

### Card 3: Chitin — Positive (Allied Standing)

**"The Spread Narrows"**

The Chitin Syndicates do something unprecedented: they open their probability models to the public. Not all of them — the Syndicates are not idiots — but the market prediction algorithms that were previously reserved for Syndicate traders. They call it "the Narrowing." Public price spreads at Chitin stations drop from +/-8% to +/-3%. Trade volume triples in a fiscal quarter.

You taught them this. Not through ideology — the Chitin do not respond to ideology — but through demonstrated returns. Your trading record, running Chitin probability models alongside open-market intuition, generated margins that outperformed every closed Syndicate desk. The Syndicates looked at the data. The data said: the pilot trades better with public information than we trade with private information. The Chitin respect data.

A young Syndicate trader at Station Verel-4 has a portrait on her console. It is not a portrait of you — the Chitin do not do portraiture. It is a probability distribution curve, annotated in the trader's own hand. The curve is yours. The shape of your trading pattern, rendered as mathematics. She is studying it the way an artist studies a master's brushwork.

The market is better now. The Chitin will claim credit. You will not mind.

### Card 4: Chitin — Negative (Hostile/Neutral Standing)

**"The Hedge Fails"**

The Chitin Syndicates hedged against the thread degradation. They built options positions on every conceivable outcome — containment, accommodation, collapse, stasis. Seven thousand derivatives contracts layered into a portfolio that was supposed to generate returns regardless of which future arrived.

The future that arrived was not on the list.

You never traded with the Chitin enough for them to build a model of your behavior. Without that model, their probability engines treated you as noise — an unpredictable variable to be hedged against rather than incorporated. When your endgame actions reshaped the galaxy's trade topology, seven thousand derivatives expired worthless in a single tick. The Chitin call it "the Correlation Event." Every position they held was correlated to the same assumption: that the thread network's structure was permanent. It was not. You proved that. They were not positioned for the proof.

The Syndicates survive. They always survive — metamorphosis is their founding metaphor, and they dissolve and rebuild the way their ancestors dissolved and rebuilt in the pupal chamber. But the rebuilding is slower this time. The old models are dead. The new models have a variable they cannot parameterize. The Chitin word for that variable translates, roughly, as "the thing we should have listened to."

## Weavers

### Card 5: Weavers — Positive (Allied Standing)

**"The Bridge at Null Point"**

The Weavers build a bridge. Not a metaphorical bridge — a physical one. A structure spanning the gap between two thread endpoints at Null Point, where a connection degraded into fracture space forty years ago. No one thought the gap could be crossed without thread infrastructure. The Weavers cross it with silk-composite lattice and accommodation-geometry reinforcement, using techniques you taught them by existing. By showing them what a hull that functions in metric-variant space looks like.

The bridge takes three years to build. Twelve Weaver construction teams working in shifts, extending the lattice one meter at a time into space where measurement cannot be trusted. They solve the measurement problem the Weaver way: they feel the vibrations. Every strand is a sensor. The bridge does not rely on coordinates. It relies on tension.

The first ship crosses on a Tuesday. A Communion scout, naturally — the Communion is always first into uncertain space. The Weaver forewoman watches from the construction platform and allows herself a gesture that Weaver culture considers deeply emotional: she places one hand on the structure and holds it there for four seconds, feeling the vibrations of the crossing ship transmitted through the lattice she built.

They named one span after you. Span 7, the one that crosses the deepest fracture. They chose the hardest section. The Weavers consider that a compliment.

### Card 6: Weavers — Negative (Hostile/Neutral Standing)

**"The Web Contracts"**

The Weavers pull back. Not dramatically — the Weavers do nothing dramatically — but steadily, over months. Thread repair contracts go unfilled. Station construction bids arrive late, with higher costs and longer timelines. The Drydock at Station Theln-3, which serviced half the galaxy's heavy haulers, closes for "structural reassessment" and does not reopen.

You did not build with them. That is all it took. The Weavers are ambush predators who build structures and wait for opportunities. You were the opportunity — the pilot with the accommodation-geometry hull, the one who could have shown them how to build in metric-variant space. You walked past the web. The web contracted.

The consequences are slow and structural, like everything the Weavers do. Ships that needed drydock service go to Concord yards instead, which charge more and take longer. Thread repairs fall behind schedule, and the connections that degrade are in Weaver territory first — the territory they are not maintaining because they are rebuilding inward, reinforcing what they have instead of extending what they could have had.

A Weaver engineer at Station Theln-3 spends her evenings working on a personal project: a scale model of a bridge she designed for a gap at Null Point. The model is exquisite. The bridge will never be built. She does not know what she is missing, exactly. But she feels the absence in the tension of every strand she lays.

## Valorin Clans

### Card 7: Valorin — Positive (Allied Standing)

**"The Burrow Holds"**

The Valorin stop expanding. This has never happened before.

It happens because you gave them something they never had: a frontier they chose instead of one they were driven to. The fracture-space maps you shared, the accommodation-geometry techniques you demonstrated, the forward bases you built in their territory and then handed over without conditions — these gave the Valorin clans options. Options they could evaluate, weigh, and decide against.

Clan Verath settles a shimmer-zone system and builds a permanent colony — the first Valorin settlement that is not a frontier outpost designed to be abandoned when the next frontier opens. They build in stone instead of prefab. They plant crops with a five-year harvest cycle. A Valorin grandmother tells her grandchildren a bedtime story about a pilot who fought alongside the clans and then gave them something more valuable than a fight: a reason to stay.

The Valorin word for "home" and the Valorin word for "trap" used to be the same word. Clan Verath coined a new one. It translates, approximately, as "the burrow you return to." The etymologists note that the new word's root structure borrows from a Concord trade term. The Valorin would be mortified to learn this. You find it funny.

The frontier is still there. The Valorin still explore it. But they come back now. That changes everything.

### Card 8: Valorin — Negative (Hostile/Neutral Standing)

**"The Swarm Disperses"**

The Valorin clans fragment. Without a unifying ally — without someone outside the clans who fought alongside them and earned Blood-Kin status — the internal politics that expansion always papered over become terminal. Clan Verath accuses Clan Dorath of hoarding frontier maps. Clan Dorath accuses Clan Verath of trading Rare Metals to the Chitin at below-market rates. Both accusations are true. Neither matters. What matters is that no one trusts anyone enough to let it go.

You fought the Valorin once. Or you ignored them. The result is the same: the clans never had a neutral party with military credibility to arbitrate internal disputes. The Valorin do not do diplomacy among themselves — they do hierarchy, established through combat. But a hierarchy of clans fighting each other is not a hierarchy. It is a brawl.

Frontier expansion continues, but it is competitive now. Two clans settle the same system and spend more resources fortifying against each other than developing the colony. A third clan raids both. The Rare Metals output from Valorin territory drops by 30% in a year, and the Chitin supply chains that depend on Valorin extraction begin to wobble.

A Valorin scout, the last survivor of a clan that no longer exists, docks at a Concord station and asks for asylum. She is nineteen. She has eleven kill-marks on her hull. She does not know another way to live. Concord takes her in, because that is what Concord does. She does not say thank you. The Valorin do not have that word either.

## Drifter Communion

### Card 9: Communion — Positive (Allied Standing)

**"The Last Threshold-Crosser"**

The Communion elder who told you the truth — "You are not the first. Every few generations, someone finds a piece of it" — retires from the waystation circuit. She settles at a shimmer-zone observatory and spends her remaining years cataloging the accommodation-geometry signatures of every threshold-crosser the Communion has recorded. There were eleven before you. She finds evidence of a twelfth, predating Communion records entirely.

You are the thirteenth. You are the first one who came back with infrastructure instead of stories. The relay nodes you built (or the array you activated, or the fortress you established — the elder does not care which path you chose, only that you chose) changed the Communion's relationship with hope. For generations, hope was an act of faith — "maybe the next threshold-crosser will go further." Now hope has an address. It is docked at Haven.

The Communion does not build monuments. They build waystations — places for travelers to rest. The newest waystation is at the edge of the shimmer zone nearest Haven. It is named in the Communion tradition: not for the person, but for the moment. The waystation's name translates as "the day the pattern changed." Communion pilots who dock there leave offerings of Exotic Crystals at the navigation shrine. The crystals hum at a frequency that matches your fracture module's accommodation signature. The Communion noticed. They always notice.

The elder's catalog, when completed, is transmitted to every waystation in the network. It is titled, simply, "Thirteen." The Communion has never transmitted a document to the entire network before. The elder says: this one needed to be shared. Everyone should know the pattern. Everyone should know it changed.

### Card 10: Communion — Negative (Hostile/Neutral Standing)

**"Fourteen"**

The Communion elder who would have told you the truth waits at a waystation you never visit. She has a speech prepared — the same speech she has given to seven threshold-crossers before you: "You are not the first." She will never give it. You dock at Communion stations for fuel and supplies, if you dock at all. You do not stay. You do not ask. The elder watches your fracture signature on the long-range sensors and adds a note to her catalog: "Thirteenth. No contact. Pattern continues."

The pattern: someone finds accommodation geometry. They go deeper. They do not build infrastructure. They go deeper. They stop transmitting. The Communion marks their last known position on the waystation charts with a symbol that means "here, someone was."

You do not disappear. That is the kindest thing that can be said about your trajectory. You complete your endgame — whichever path you chose — without the Communion's navigation data, without their shimmer-zone frequencies, without the accumulated wisdom of a species that has been watching threshold-crossers for centuries. You succeed anyway, because you are competent and stubborn and lucky. But the Communion's data would have told you things. The shimmer-zone frequencies would have reduced your hull stress by 40%. The elder's speech would have prepared you for what the metric does to pilots who go deep without understanding what they are becoming.

The elder adds a final note: "Thirteenth. Survived. Did not understand why." She closes the catalog. She opens a new file. She labels it "Fourteen." The Communion has been doing this for a very long time. They will keep doing it. Hope is patient. The threshold-crossers are not.
